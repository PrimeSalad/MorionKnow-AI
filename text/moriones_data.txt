TAGALOG, MARINDUQUE ISLAND PROVINCE, CENTRAL PHILIPPINES.
This is a street performance done by the Tagalog of Marinduque province. It is a Christian Lenten season practice.

FROM HOLY WEDNESDAY to Easter Sunday, male penitents from the towns of Gasan, Mogpog, Boac and Santa Cruz march the streets wearing personal variations of the Roman soldier dress. A distinct feature of the dress is a morion (mask), which is a painted wooden head cover consisting of a frowning face and a Roman helmet. On Good Friday, the masked penitents (moriones) dramatize the kalbariohan – portions of the story of the Passion of Christ. It is a street play similar to the cenaculo or sinakulo and starts at high noon. On Easter Sunday, the moriones stage the pugutan – the beheading of the Roman centurion, Longinus – who suffered this fate at the hands of the Roman soldiers because of his conversion to Christianity.

A morion participates in this ritual as a vow or as a form of penance. The reasons for doing it varies for each person – thanksgiving for being cured from an ailment (either the morion himself or a member of his family), petition for good health and bountiful harvest, or simply devoutness in the faith.

By donning the mask and the uniform of a Roman soldier, a morion believes that he participates in the passion of Christ. With Holy Week in the Philippines falling usually during the summer months (March or April) with temperatures ranging from 30–32 degrees Celsius (86–89.6 degrees Fahrenheit) and the humidity at about 71%, it is extremely difficult for a morion to walk around the town the whole day for about five days.

The features of a morion mask help when the penitent walks around the streets, either on his own or as part of a group. Like in any play, a group of moriones simulates a sword duel and general mayhem. Being incognito, they are subjected to taunts from passersby, specifically children, who they scare off in turn with grunts. Aside from a sword, a morion usually holds a pair of wood sticks called kalutang which he beats as he walks around the streets. The sound made by the kalutang signals that a morion is roaming around in the vicinity.

The traditional form of the pugutan was a free-ranging search and chase of Longinus through the streets of the town, up the trees and so on, until he is finally caught. Before being executed, he declaims a prayer from the Pasyon Generis. The mask of Longinus always has one eye closed.

Morion masks are called different names depending on size and design: there is the Moriong Bastos, Moriong Tanga, Moriong Bungo, Moriong Taas Panga, Moriong Bingi, Moriong Taas Binti and others.

In the 1970s, the town of Gasan introduced changes to the performance through a scripted play by the writer, Celso al. Carunungan, that now uses a stage, taped dialogue and background music. Now, there are re-enactments of biblical scenes. Other contemporary changes have been added.

EXPLANATORY NOTE

The Philippines is recognized as one of the few predominantly Christian nations in Asia, with its Christian heritage tracing back to 1521, when Ferdinand Magellan arrived on Philippine shores. This event marked the beginning of the widespread conversion of the indigenous population to Christianity.

Throughout history, the Philippines has become known not only for its faith but also for its vibrant and deeply rooted celebrations of Christian culture and traditions. Among these, the observance of Holy Week holds significant religious and cultural importance. In the Province of Marinduque, the Moriones Festival has been celebrated since 1807, introduced by Father Dionisio Santiago, the Parish Priest of Mogpog. This festival is commemorated annually during Holy Week, featuring participants dressed in colorful costumes and masks resembling Roman soldiers.

The Moriones Festival serves as an act of penance and a form of atonement for sins by the people of Marinduque, dramatizing the biblical story of Saint Longinus, a Roman soldier who was healed of blindness after being touched by the blood of Jesus when he pierced Christ’s side with a spear. This powerful narrative is reenacted by the people of Marinduque as a meaningful expression of faith and devotion.

Since the festival originated in Marinduque and has become an integral part of the local culture and identity, it is fitting to officially recognize the province as the home of the Moriones Festival. Such recognition would honor the heritage of the people of Marinduque and further promote pride in their unique cultural tradition.

In view of the foregoing, the approval of this bill is earnestly requested.

AN ACT DECLARING THE PROVINCE OF MARINDUQUE AS THE OFFICIAL HOME OF THE MORIONES FESTIVAL AND PROVIDING FOR ITS PROMOTION AND PRESERVATION

Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representative of the Philippines in Congress assembled:

SECTION 1. It is. In line with the policy of the State to protect, preserve and promote the country’s historical and cultural heritage, the Province of Marinduque is hereby officially recognized and declared as the Home of the Moriones Festival.

SECTION 2. The Department of Tourism (DOT), in coordination with local government units and cultural agencies, shall actively promote the Moriones Festival as a cultural attraction in the national tourism agenda, ensuring its preservation and enhancement for future generations.

SECTION 3. This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in the Official Gazette or in a newspaper of general circulation.
